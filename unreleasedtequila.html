Development overview

Note: This game is currently under NDA, so I won't be able to discuss specific details about my work that reveal what the game is actually like.

As a direct translation from publicly available information provided by Tequila Works:

"The Ancient Mariner is an open-world narrative action adventure developed by Tequila Works, focused on human emotions as the driving force of gameplay.
With an introspective approach, the player must reconnect with their childhood and learn to understand and channel complex emotions in order to progress.
The game relies on a systematic emotional system, free exploration, and artistic direction with strong symbolic elements."

I began working on this project from its very early concept phase and continued until production was halted. Throughout the development process, I transitioned between various roles as the organization adapted to different phases of development.

Following a brief conceptual prototype phase as the Technical Lead, I transitioned into the dual roles of Game Designer and Product Owner for the majority of the development process.

Conceptual Prototypes phase: Technical Lead

I began working during the conceptual phase as the lead on the technical aspects of gameplay, with support from the programming lead, who was primarily focused on wrapping up other projects at the time.

My initial priority was to define the project's architecture, guided by these key principles:

  • Flexibility to allow for experimentation with new mechanics and quick iteration.
  • Exploration of new engine features, such as the Gameplay Ability System (GAS) or the Enhanced Input System, to better support a larger-scale game.
  • Leveraging existing technologies developed by Tequila to enhance the features being introduced.

The most important factor during this phase was flexibility and fast iteration, as we needed to develop a concept prototype on a tight schedule and explore many ideas. This meant that the initial programming foundation would likely be replaced later with a more stable and sustainable system for a larger team once the game entered full production.

That said, we aimed to write high-quality code that could be reused as much as possible in later phases. This approach proved successful, as many of the core ideas, implementations, and adaptations from this phase were used as a foundation in later development, contributing to a smooth transition into full production.

In addition to defining the architecture, I led a small team of 2-3 junior programmers, introducing them to Tequila's codebase and ensuring the quality and maintainability of their work. I was also directly involved in implementing several key components for the prototype, including player movement, gameplay abilities (for both players and NPCs), respawning systems, damage, UI, collisions, and more. Essentially, many of the building blocks for a fully functional and playable prototype.

This phase of development was highly collaborative, with frequent meetings and discussions between the programming and design teams as we experimented with various ideas.

Production phase: Game Designer

As the game entered production, I transitioned into the role of Game Designer, where I was primarily responsible for the 3Cs: Character, Controller, and Camera, drawing on my experience from previous projects.

The character design encompassed many aspects of the game, so it was divided into different gameplay loops, each assigned to different designers. Beyond basic character features, I was responsible for one of these loops, centered around character movement. By the time development halted, this gameplaly loop was highly regarded as one of the most fun and engaging aspects of the game.

In my role as a Game Designer, my responsibilities often included:

  • Writing Game Design Documents (GDDs): These varied in length and complexity depending on the feature's development phase. The implementation team praised these GDDs for their clarity and detailed explanations, which led to efficient implementations with minimal iterations needed to achieve optimal results.
  • Brainstorming and Feedback Sessions: I actively participated in brainstorming sessions and feedback rounds with my design peers to generate interesting ideas, solve gameplay issues, and refine our designs. I was known for making significant contributions that were appreciated by both leads and teammates.
  • Showcasing Design Ideas: I created presentations, videos, documents, and prototypes to clearly communicate design solutions to the Lead Game Designer or Game Direction as needed.
  • Collaboration: I worked closely with other game designers, programmers, animators, and artists in small teams to develop specific features.
  • Adjusting and Balancing Game Parameters: My goal was to enhance the quality and feel of various game features through careful tuning.
  • Integrating Animations: I integrated character gameplay animations and tied them into the game’s logic events.
  • Creating Example Levels: These served as showcases for the potential of my assigned game features, demonstrating their possibilities.
  • Reviewing Work: I regularly reviewed my designs and progress with the assigned Lead Game Designer to ensure alignment and quality.

In addition to the 3Cs, I was also responsible for designing various isolated features as needed, including specific game objects and UI elements.

Production phase: Product Owner

Shortly after the game entered production, I was assigned the co-role of Product Owner within the overall Agile Scrum organization. This role was chosen for me due to my communication skills, comprehensive understanding of the development process, and long-term vision for features and implementations.

My primary objective was to maintain continuous communication with both Production and Game Direction, enabling me to coordinate a team comprising programmers, designers, and artists. This coordination ensured the quality of deliveries and their alignment with direction requirements.

Thanks to my reputation for delivering high-quality implementations and designs in similar features, I was assigned to the team focused on player-related features. This included elements directly controlled by the player, such as the main character, camera, UI, among others. It was the largest team, growing to over 20 members before combat features were split off into a separate team to maintain manageability.

In more detail, my responsibilities as a Product Owner involved:

  • Communicating with Game Direction to understand the goals for my team's assigned features in the medium to long term.
  • Organizing tasks with Production broadly for each delivery cycle, ensuring that features met expected outcomes while adhering to budget constraints.
  • Coordinating individual tasks within the team for every sprint, fine-tuning them to align with overall goals.
  • Defining acceptance criteria for tasks to ensure they matched direction requirements and expectations.
  • Reviewing completed tasks to verify adherence to acceptance criteria, addressing misunderstandings as necessary.
  • Resolving team doubts about feature developments, arranging meetings with Game Direction when needed.
  • Adapting sprints in collaboration with Production to accommodate unexpected issues or development changes.
  • Communicating with other Product Owners whenever dependencies between our respective teams emerged.
  • Collecting and providing constructive feedback regarding team organization and processes to Production for process improvements.
  • Compiling progress reports on the development made by the team each sprint, sharing them with the rest of the studio.

Being a Product Owner was both a challenging and rewarding role that required increasing attention as the team and project complexity grew. Despite the challenges, the team consistently delivered high-quality features on time, resulting in highly sought-after content for the game.
